# The Most Expensive Word in Engineering: 'Just'
#### 22 July 2026

whenever someone starts a sentence with a "can you just...", every engineer instinctively checks how much free time they no longer have.

every engineer has heard the word.

"can you just move that hole a little?" 

"can you just make it lighter?" 

"can you just add AI?"

on paper, these sound harmless. they never take more than a sentence to describe. unfortunately, engineering has never charged by the number of words.

---

## "just" hides work
the problem with 'just' isn't the word itself. it's that it describes only the visible outcome.

to someone looking at a CAD model, moving a hole is exactly that: moving a hole.

to an engineer, however, that single change might mean reviewing manufacturability, updating drawings, checking clearances, rerunning simulations, verifying tolerances and making sure nothing else quietly broke along the way.

the hole 'just' moved 5 millimetres. the workload moved considerably more.

software engineers hear the word just as often. "can we just add a button here?"

adding the button is probably the easiest part. 

the button needs somewhere to send data. that data needs validation. someone has to handle errors. permissions have to be checked. the interface needs to be updated. the backend needs testing and eventually someone has to document why the button exists in the first place.

the button took five minutes. everything around the button took five hours.

---

## we only notice the visible part
humans are remarkably good at compressing complicated work into a single sentence. we see the result, not the process.

a car drives. a website loads. a machines finishes a part.

by the time we see any of it, thousands of decisions have already been made. the smoother something appears to work, the easier it is to believe it was simple to build.

that's why "just" exists. it isn't usually dismissive. it's incomplete.

---

## engineers think in dependencies
engineering isn't just solving problems. it's predicting consequences.

changing one dimension isn't one decision. it's a chain of decisions.

experienced engineers don't immediately think about the change itself. they think about everything connected to it. move a hole and assembly might change. increase accuracy and cost might increase. fix one bug and another system might stop working.

engineering is less about individual components and more about the relationships between them.

---

## "just" is a compliment in disguise
strangely enough, hearing the word "just" can actually mean you've done your job well.

relating to my previous post, good engineering hides complexity. nobody notices the testing when the software never crashes or the tolerances when everything fits together or the calculations when the bridge stays standing.

they only see something that appears effortless. the better engineered a product is, the easier it looks. 

perhaps that's why people believe changing it shoudl be easy too.
